Introduction to Confidence Intervals

Carson Sievert

Re-Cap

  1. Provided that I know the population mean and variance. I can specify the mean and variance of the sampling distribution of the sample mean: X̄ ∼ (μ, σ2 / n).
  • Note that the shape of X̄ will be at least approximately normal unless our population distribution is non-normal and n < 30.
  1. With this information, I can find any proportion of the curve that I want, for example P(X̄ < 5).
  • I can interpret this quantity as the probability the sample of size n yields a sample mean lower than 5.

Re-Cap (part 2)

  1. I can also find any percentile(s) of interest.
  • For example, suppose P(3 < X̄ < 5) = . 95
  • In this case, I know that there is a 95% chance of observing a sample mean between 3 and 5.

Transitioning to C.I.’s

  1. In reality, it is very rare that we know the population mean μ. Most of statistics is inference. That is, we infer (or esimate) plausible values for μ based on observation(s) x̄.

  2. As a result, we really have no idea what the true sampling distribution looks like!

  3. However, it is helpful to remember that our samples are taken from the true distribution!!!

Interpret the graphics

  1. The red shading defines the middle 95% of the true sampling distribution. The solid red line is the value of μ

  2. The blue and green versions are similar, except we pretend that μ is the observed sample mean x̄.

  3. Notice how both the blue and green shaded portions overlap with the solid red line (μ). Thus, in both cases, it just so happens that the 95% confidence interval contains μ.
